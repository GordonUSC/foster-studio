The Whole
Instrument.

Forty-some machines. One room. One hand.

You sent me twenty pictures and called it a collection. It is not a collection. A collection is a shelf of things that happen to be near each other. What you have built is one instrument, with an analog keybed and a modular voice and a soldering iron and a jazz-theory shelf, all wired toward the same two feet of desk. I want to walk it with you the way it deserves to be walked. As a room, not a shopping list.

01 What you have assembled

Seven jobs, one studio.

Read the room by what each group is for, and the whole thing stops looking like gear acquisition and starts looking like a plan. Performance keyboards you play and sing on. Experimental voices you sculpt with. A rhythm section for the floor. A modular playground that plays itself. A mastering chain that finishes records. A bench where you fix and build. And a bookshelf that tells me you study form, not just buy it. None of it is casual. All of it belongs.

Performance keyboards · the three-flagship stack

The front of the room, where you play and sing.

Three flagship polysynths, all released in the last two years, is not redundancy. It is three different ways to put your hands on sound: analog warmth, hybrid texture, and expressive pitch that moves under your fingers. Stack them and you have a performance instrument no single keyboard can be.

Moog Muse · 8-voice analog, 61 keys
Moog's first true polysynth since the Memorymoog. This is the warm heart of the stack, the voice that sings on top of everything.
Waldorf Quantum MK2 · hybrid, 61 keys
Wavetable, particle, kernel, resonator, analog filter. The texture engine. Where you go when a sound has to feel like weather.
Expressive E Osmose 49 · MPE, Haken EaganMatrix
A poly-aftertouch keybed that bends and breathes per note. This is the expression instrument, the one that plays like a cello section.
Sound design · experimental voices

The instruments that make non-song music.

These are not for melodies. They are for dread, drone, breath, and grit. The texture layer under a score, or the whole point of an ambient piece.

SOMA Lyra-8 · organismic synth
Eight voices that fight each other into a living drone. A cult instrument for exactly one thing, done better than anything else.
Moog Spectravox · 10-band vocoder / spectral
Lives on the marble mantel. A whole spectral FX chain in a Mother-32 footprint.
Erica Synths Pērkons HD-01 · 4-voice hybrid
A drum machine that thinks it is a synth. The Latvian menace at the edge of rhythm and noise.
Erica Synths SteamPipe · physical modeling
Built with 112dB. Breath and pipe, an acoustic voice that no wavetable can fake.
Elektron Digitone II · FM groovebox
The sequencer-brain that can also make cold, glassy FM. A workstation hiding as a box.
Groove · the rhythm section

Everything the floor is made of.

Four boxes that cover the entire lineage of dance rhythm: the 808, the 303, a modern Roland, and a boutique acid bass. This is the engine room for the bass music you are teaching yourself right now.

Behringer RD-8 · 808 clone, full size
The kick and clap that every low-end record is built on.
Behringer TD-3-MO · 303, Devilfish-modded
Acid, with the modifications that make it scream. The squelch you cannot get any other way.
Roland TR-6S · modern Rhythm Composer
The compact modern brain when you want clean sampled drums fast.
Erica Synths Bassline DB-01 · analog acid bass
A second, fatter acid line so the low end has two voices arguing.
Modular · the playground that plays itself

A generative instrument, plus a whole second Doepfer rack behind it.

This is the part most people never get to. A boutique two-row case full of the best small-format modules made, backed by a full Doepfer A-100 system for utility. Between Marbles for controlled randomness and Pam's for clock, this rig can compose without you. You set the rules, it plays the piece. And the ES-9 means all of it talks to the Mac Mini as cleanly as a plugin.

Mastering chain · where records get finished

An SSL front to back. This is the part that says you finish things.

Anyone can buy synths. Very few build a real mastering chain at home. You did. A Fusion for the master bus, a SiX desk to sum on, an Alpha VHD preamp array, X-Rack, Alpha-Link conversion, plus boutique color from Bakelab, Warm Audio, and that hot-pink Bébé Chérie on a milk crate. The last one is the tell: you care about the sound of the summing, not just the notes.

SSL Fusion · stereo master processor
Vintage Drive, Violet EQ, HF Compressor, Stereo Image. The last box before the record is real.
SSL SiX · SuperAnalogue desk
On a footstool, ready to sum the whole room into one stereo bus with real analog glue.
SSL Alpha VHD Pre · X-Rack · Alpha-Link · preamp + conversion
Eight channels of red-knob preamp, a SuperAnalogue channel, and clean MADI conversion. A proper front end.
Bakelab Wizard · Warm Audio WA-73 · color
A Polish valve saturator and a Neve 1073 clone. This is where warmth gets added on purpose.
OTO Machines Bébé Chérie · 6-channel analog mixer
Hot pink, COMP and BLISS onboard, sitting on an overturned milk crate. The most Foster object in the room.
Ferrofish · iConnectivity · TC Electronic · Furman · glue
Conversion, MIDI routing, effects, and clean power. The unglamorous parts that make everything else usable.

02 The room, imagined

How the sound moves.

Here is the room assembled, not as a pile but as a signal path. Everything the machines make flows the same direction, left to right, from voices to summing to master to capture. The modular and the grooveboxes and the three keyboards all pour into the SiX. The SiX sums to the Fusion. The Fusion prints to the Mac Mini. One clock, from Pam's, holds the whole thing in time. That is what makes forty machines a studio instead of forty machines.

04 What is missing, and what is next

Honest, and restrained.

The room is close to complete, so this is short on purpose. A few things I could not see from photos, and a few small proposals that fit the soul of what you built. Nothing here is a shopping list. If a piece does not make the room more itself, it does not belong on this page.

open question

Room treatment

I cannot tell from the photos. A room this beautiful, sage stripes and marble mantel, may or may not be treated for sound. If the SSL chain is going to earn its keep, the room has to be honest first. Worth telling me what the acoustics are actually doing.

already covered

Monitoring

Settled. The pair on the stands are ADAM Audio, gold ribbon tweeters and all, one on each side. That ribbon is known for high-frequency accuracy, which is exactly what you want at the end of the SSL Fusion chain. Nothing to spend here. The monitoring already matches the room.

proposal

A mic to match the pop filter

There is a pop filter on a gooseneck in vocal position, but I did not see a microphone worth that filter, or that preamp. A single good large-diaphragm condenser would let the WA-73 and the Alpha VHD finally do what they are there for.

worth an opinion

The sequencer question

Pam's runs the clock and the Digitone can sequence, so you may not need more. But if you ever want a single hardware brain to arrange the whole room without the Mac, that is the one gap I would think about. Your call, and I would want to hear how you sequence now.

proposal

Something for the hands

A Push 3 or a MidiFighter Twister would give you tactile control over the modular and the Mac without reaching for a mouse. Small, but it keeps you playing the instrument instead of operating a computer. Only if it fits how you actually work.

probably fine

Tracking to the Mac Mini

You are almost certainly recording direct to the Mac Mini through the ES-9 and the Alpha-Link, and that is completely fine. A Mac Mini is more than enough for this. No need to change anything here. I just wanted to close the loop honestly.

The mantel principle

Add almost nothing. Play what is there.

The truth is you do not need much. A pair of speakers you trust and a microphone you love, and this room is finished. The rest of the list above is optional. The real next step is not a purchase. It is pressing record on any one of the seven records already sitting in the room.

05 The room, seen with love

The room is you.

A note, from Gordon

Fos, I have known you since we were sophomores. Forty years. You moved across the country to help me build a career, then went home to build your own life, and somewhere in there I got to hand you EDM at Ultra and watch you fall for it the way we all do. And now this. A room.

Here is what I see when I look at your twenty pictures. There is a baby stroller folded in one frame, and a Trapper Keeper in the pink-striped alcove, and a bookshelf where Jazzology sits next to The Nature of the Violin. There is a Moog Muse three feet from that shelf. There is a hot-pink Bébé Chérie sitting on an overturned milk crate, next to an SSL chain that costs more than some cars. There is a Tektronix scope on the desk because you do not just buy the modules, you build them.

None of that fits together on a spreadsheet. All of it fits together in you. A man who reads violin acoustics and owns a soldering iron. A portfolio manager who teaches himself bass music at fifty-five. Someone who names himself after a toad-riding sage and then actually collects the woodblock prints of the artist who drew him. The room is not a collection because you are not a collection. You are all of it at once, and the room is just the part I can finally see.

So do not let it sit there looking expensive. It is not a museum. It is an instrument, and it is waiting for your hands. Press record. Make the ugly first pass. Then make the good one. I will be the first to hear it, the way I have been first to a lot of things with you for forty years.

The room is you, Fos. Now go play it. Gordon · LYMI
